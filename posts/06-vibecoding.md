Back home in HK, I never really had the chance to attend many hackathons.

Realistically, there was just one major one - the Cathay Hackathon.

I still _very_ vividly remember our first win, as well as what our product actually looked like.

At the time, I was still just barely learning React, AI coding tools were still pretty crap, and HK didn't even have access to ChatGPT yet, so I was getting rate-limited on [Poe](https://poe.com/) half the time anyway.

Looking back, our project looked _awful_ - but it also felt way more _real_.

### In memoriam

Since starting my degree at Warwick, I've had the chance to attend a lot more hackathons, and they've all exposed me to the same harsh truth:

Hackathons feel... _weird_ now.

A lot of them don't really feel like engineering competitions anymore - they feel like contests of who can pump the most tokens through Claude in 24 hours.

And look, I'm not anti-AI. I use AI literally every day. I think it's an incredible tool, and it's obviously here to stay.

But I think we've slowly started treating it less like a tool and more like the solution itself.

Walk around demo day at basically any hackathon and I swear every landing page looks identical.

Purple gradients, smooth animations, punchy AI-generated slogan. More recently, there's been this weird shift toward the whole orange/brown minimalist serif-font aesthetic (that's MY territory!), a la Anthropic.

You can almost predict the stack before someone even opens the demo.

Next.js frontend, maybe a Supabase backend, and it's almost always some AI wrapper with a vaguely productivity-themed pitch.

Most of these projects didn't even feel _built_. They felt assembled.

### I'm convinced we can do better

The thing is, I genuinely think we can write better software in roughly the same timeframe.

A lot of the time, the AI-generated code I see at hackathons has this very obvious "patchwork" feeling to it - like the model brute-forced its way into solving a problem through sheer volume instead of actually understanding the system.

You end up with bizarre abstractions, weird architectural decisions, and massive amounts of unnecessary complexity solving what should've been a pretty simple issue.

The funny thing is that if you took the same amount of iteration and planning people spend prompting Claude, you could probably build something cleaner, safer, and more maintainable yourself.

Maybe not as flashy, sure.

But definitely more intentional.

### AI as a tool

Again, AI itself isn't the problem.

I just think we need to be a lot more conscious about _how_ we use it.

To me, AI should be the new rubber duck.

Argue with it. Bounce ideas off it. Ask if your code is idiomatic. Discuss architecture. Generate scaffolding. Use it to accelerate the boring stuff.

But at some point, you still need engineering judgment.

After all, the hard part of software engineering was never just typing code into a file.
