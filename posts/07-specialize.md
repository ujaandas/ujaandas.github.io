Genuine question. As a soon-to-be graduate, this is a question I ask myself a lot.

**Should we specialize, or generalize our skillset?**

### For specialization

Obviously, you hit a smaller niche, and face less competition. Usually, this is in an area you can't just "learn", like VLSI, robotics or embedded.

But equally as obviously, you hit a smaller market.

The only companies that _really_ have the ability to invest in these things and the infrastructure thereof are far and few between.

### For generalization

You can apply to a whole heap of more jobs. Any and everything with "software" or "development" in the title is fair game.

But, and especially so in recent years, the bar of entry isn't just a CS degree. You need experience with the company's stack, tooling, workflows, etc...

How can you prove your competence?

Jack of all trades, master of none, right?

### So, now what?

Honestly, I don't really know. 

I kind of fall into the second bucket. 

I've interned at HCI and program analysis research labs, worked on backend, frontend _and_ AI features at startups, and all my projects are in C++ or Go. I'm explicitly interested in compilers and PL (i.e. completely useless as a marketable skill). 

My "stack" is all over the place.

But maybe the question isn't whether we should specialize or generalize.

Maybe it's whether we can build enough depth to matter, while staying adaptable enough to survive.

AI kind of forces the issue. The more software becomes commoditized, the more valuable real taste, domain knowledge, and first-principles understanding become. 

At the same time, the people who seem to thrive are rarely confined to one narrow lane - they can move between systems, fields, and abstractions quickly.

So I don't know if being "all over the place" is actually a weakness anymore.

Maybe early on, it's the point.
